This month in... The One '94

Posted on 30 June 2020 20:02 NZST by Michael Keith

Category: This month in...

News

News broke as the mag went to press that Commodore worldwide had basically filed for administration in the Bahamas, where the company was registered. Just what this meant was unclear, but things did not look good. It wasn’t terribly surprising considering the losses the company had posted over the last couple of years, though Commodore UK had performed well. While this move was obviously concerning, The One were confident that the continued popularity of the Amiga would mean a positive outcome one way or another.

The winners of the ECTS awards were announced with “Doom” dominating, picking up “Computer Game of the Year” and “Overall Game of the Year”. Viewers of BBC kids TV show “Alive and Kicking” chose “Frontier” for their award, “Rebel Assault” won “Best CD Game” and “Syndicate” “Most Original Game”. Perhaps the most galling win as far as Amiga fans were concerned was that of the Atari Jaguar over the CD32 for “Best Hardware”.

Mersyside-base Spectacular Graphics had produced a coin-op cabinet for the arcades that could house a CD32.

BT joined the games industry’s governing body ELPSA as they continued their push towards delivering games and movies over their network. While it was clear this would be some way off for the customers, they were pumping billions of pounds into their efforts and had setup an experiment with 70 employees in Ipswich. Oracle were providing their “Massively Parallel Processing” tech which would allow BT to keep just one digitised copy of game while serving thousands of players.

The CD32 continued to dominate CD software sales and several new titles were announced: “Rise of the Robots”, “Cyberwar” (not released?), “Empire Soccer” and “Sim City 2000”.

The World of Commodore show was making a comeback, slightly re-branded as World of Amiga and would take place at Wembley in November.

The CD sales figures put the CD32 at a 55.6% market share, PC 24.2%, Mega CD 18.3% and CD-i 1.1%.

Team 17 were about to release “Alien Breed II: Tower Assault” on floppy as well as a CD32 enhanced “Alien Breed II”. Other titles in development were an arcade strategy game called “King of Thieves” and a 10-pin bowling game.

The One were launching a club.. maybe? Or maybe it was just an excuse to explain away typos as coded messages.

Another month, another new CD32 bundle from Commodore. The Spectacular Voyage pack featured six games: “Microcosm”, “The Chaos Engine”, “Wing Commander”, “Dangerous Streets”, “Diggers” and “Oscar”. The price was ¬£249.99.

“P.I.D.” (Private Investigator Dollarlaly) was to be a racing game from Terramarque in the “Spy Hunter”/“APB” vein. It was unreleased.

Tucked away in a corner of Commodore’s ECTS stand was a prototype CD drive for the A1200 which caused a bit of a stir. However, there were no concrete plans for selling it at that point in time. David Pleasance felt the lack of MPEG video playback would reduce its appeal so they would need to find a compelling price point.

Developer Stellar Genesis Agency were working on CD strategy game “Space Academy” for Mindscape (unreleased?).

Speaking of Mindscape… they had just been bought by media giant Pearson for a cool ¬£310 million. Pearson owned the likes of Thames TV, the Financial Times and Penguin Books.

POSH Marketing had developed a touchscreen for the CD32 which would find its way into information access terminals like those found at airports.

The Computer Graphics Expo was to take place at Wembley in November.

New Tek’s Video Toaster was finally going to get a UK release. It wouldn’t come cheap, mind you, as the software, A4000 and NTSC -> PAL converter would total around ¬£12,000. There was also a brief mention of Commodore’s latest machine, the A4000 tower ( the “most powerful Amiga ever”) for ¬£2999.99.

Dr Julia Rutkowska told the British Psychological Society that computer games can increase sociability rather than the commonly perceived idea that they resulted in loners.

It looked like Virgin Interactive Entertainment was being fought over by PolyGram and Disney. VIE was about to be listed on the US stock exchange and was part owned by Blockbuster Video… who PolyGram had a majority stake in. Meanwhile the software house had a close relationship with Disney. At any rate, between that and the share flotation it was expected that Virgin would be getting a serious cash injection.

Computer store chain Silica (described as “the Amiga’s most loyal high street supporter”) were to open 20 new shops.

Pinball was to meet RPGs in “Ultimate Pinball Quest” from Infogrammes.

Peter Molyneux was to make an appearance at Level One in London to show off “Theme Park”.

A sequel to Sensible Software’s “Canon Fodder” was in the works, but it was early days (one level set in the desert) and they hadn’t decided on a name but were determined it wouldn’t be called “Cannon Fodder 2”.
