This month in... The One '94

Posted on 29 September 2020 20:39 NZDT by Michael Keith

Category: This month in...

News

After reporting that the saga of Commodore’s fate would be resolved “any day now” for the past few months, The One decided to quit guessing the timeline. In the latest twist, word arrived Commodore UK (lead by David Pleasance and Colin Proudfoot) were now the favourites with their proposal of a Management Buy Out, with Samsung apparently still keen. At the US head office Mehdi Ali was officially out.

The first bundle from Maxis would see “Sim City Classic”, “Sim Life” and “Sim Ant” packaged together for Â£34.99. Due for release at the same time were “A Train” and “Sim Earth”, which would be making their way to the Amiga, however it was unclear if their next two games (“SimTower” and “SimTown”) would.

Krisalis struck a deal with the PFA (Professional Footballers’ Association) to use the names of all their players and its “properties”. The first game to take advantage of this was to be “Player of the Year” and the software house re-affirmed its commitment to the Amiga. However, it doesn’t look like it saw a release.

A comic based on “Street Fighter II” was being launched by Manga Publishing.

The Live ‘94 show was returning to Earls Court in London from the 20th of September until the 25th.

Six of EA’s recent console games were getting ports to the Amiga courtesy of Ocean: “Jungle Strike”, “Skitchin’” (unreleased), “Shaq Fu”, “PGA Euro Golf”, “NHL 95” (unreleased) and “Mutant League Hockey” (unreleased). Ocean were also handling the SNES ports. Sales director Paul Patterson said: “We believe it’s important to send a clear message to the Amiga public that we will continue to support them with new and exciting products for so long as the market remains active.”

A survey of the British public by Mintel found that attitudes towards games had improved considerably over the previous few years.

The One poked some fun at Sensible Software with an old photo they had dug up of Chris Yates, Martin Galway and Jon Hare.

Mindscape was to show off Logitech’s new “Cyberman” control system in their racing game, “Mega Race” (unreleased). The “Cyberman” would provide “dimensional control” (whatever that is) and a “rattle and roll” effect.

Despite all the doom and gloom surrounding the Amiga, software sales were still strong with 21.6% vs 19.6% Mega Drive and PC 15%, though the lead was narrowing. The CD32 had slipped from almost 50% in April to 34.9% vs 44.6% PC.

Retailer Silica were introducing an extended 5 year warranty on Amigas.

The latest promotional campaign from MicroProse involved giving away a “long sleeved MicroProse sports shirt” to anyone buying a best-selling game at a number of high street chains.

Industry body ELPSA published a pocket guide for parents to try to assuage concerns about games. Meanwhile they decided to continue their stance against including full commercial games on mag coverdisks (Public Domain and shareware were fine).

Features

On this month’s two cover disks:

  • “Ruff ‘N’ Tumble” Renegade (demo)
  • “Putty Squad” System 3 (demo)
  • “Dragonstone” Core Design (demo)

The One challenged a bunch of Amiga devs to the “1994 Brainiest Programmer in Britain Quiz” which posed questions like “What should register d0 contain if trying to open the graphics.library for an A1200-specific program?” and “What is the name of the extra chip resident in the Amiga CD32?”. The winner was Andrew Braybrook who scored 70%, just ahead of Shaun Southern and Mike Montgomery with 66%. Bringing up the rear was Julian Jameson (aka Jools) with an impressive 0%.

Andy Nuttall journeyed to Llandudno in Wales to interview the Magnetic Fields crew (Shaun Southern, Andrew Morris, Peter Liggett and Doug Braisby).

Getting the Work in Progress treatment this month were:

  • “Cyberwar” SCI Southampton/SCI (unreleased?)
  • “All Terrain Racing” Team 17
  • “Football Glory” Croteam/Black Legend
  • “Litil Divil” Gremlin Ireland/Gremlin Graphics
  • “King of Thieves” Team 17 (unreleased)
  • “The Big End” The Hidden/Supervision

PD games reviewed:

Notable Reviews

  • “Universe” Core - 87% “Like ‘Flashback’, the lack of any background knowledge makes surviving in an alien environment pretty tough and the constant changing of pace ensures you’re always on your toes. Some of the links in the plot are a bit tenuous, and the solutions to a couple of the puzzles a little obscure, but the whole thing hangs together because it’s surprising.”
  • “Pinkie” Millennium - 81% “Technically, Pinkie is brilliant. The graphics are gorgeous and full of character, with enough humorous animation to keep you amused for ages. There are an impressive amount of levels to plough through (something that - happily - seems to be becoming a Millennium trademark) and each of the worlds is satisfyingly different from the last.”
  • “On the Ball” Ascon Sports/Daze - 90% “It’s rare to find a game that has both the depth of detail that most management game players go for AND the graphic quality to make it interesting enough to watch, so that you don’t just sit back and wait for text results.”
  • “Ishar 3” Silmarils/Daze - 86% “Ishar 3 is an improvement over its prequels, but it still suffers slightly from the ‘wandering’ syndrome, so that the chances are if you can’t find what you’re looking for quickly, you’ll give up altogether. If you do persevere, though, you’ll discover something of a challenge - and that, above anything else, is what RPG afficionados [sic] are after.”
  • “The Clue” Neo/Black Legend - 88% “The learning curve is spot-on, and you’ll soon get used to the different aspects of planning and strategy, not to mention making things difficult for Johnny Pig (you mean the police, yes? - Simon). The best part is the planning (which can be saved and re-run to check fine details) and the going into the burglary, running the plan, and sitting back while your outfit does its best to make you a fortune!”
