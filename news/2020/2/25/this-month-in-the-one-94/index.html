This month in... The One '94

Posted on 25 February 2020 19:28 NZDT by Michael Keith

Category: This month in...

News

Editor David Upchurch saw a bright 1994 ahead for the Amiga.

Word on the street was that Commodore UK were close to striking a deal with LucasArts that would see them bring their PC hits “Rebel Assault” and “Day of the Tentacle” to the CD32. (It didn’t happen)

While England had suffered the shame of failing to qualify for the upcoming World Cup, Virgin were keen to join with Adidas to sponsor a new initiative to promote youth footballing skills.

The main Commodore news this month was the release of the full motion video cartridge for the CD32, which they hoped would bring more customers to the machine by offering MPEG1 movies and music videos. The add-on cost a cool Â£199.99. They also took out their famous “To Be This Good Will Take Sega Ages” billboard right across from Sega’s UK HQ.

Given all the doom and gloom surrounding the Amiga, it was interesting to note that the Amiga remained the number one games computer over the Christmas period, accounting for 57.5% of all software sold. The PC was gaining, however. The biggest titles were “Mortal Kombat”, “Frontier” and “Cannon Fodder” with Virgin raking in an impressive 15% of the market by itself.

109 CD32s had been installed in the London Transport Museum, delivering text, animation, video and sound fx for a touch screen experience.

With the rumblings of a government imposed ratings scheme on the horizon, Alternative took the initiative and had many of its own games classified with a Uc label, signifying they were suitable for children. They also updated “Jetstrike” and now claimed that it was the first ever “never ending computer game” thanks to additional missions/weapons/data supplied via magazine coverdisks etc. Hmm.

System 3 were to releases five of their Amiga titles for the CD32: “Putty”, “International Karate”, “Last Ninja 3”, “Flimbo’s Quest” and “Myth”.

Virgin Interactive had spun out Virgin Sound and Vision which would focus on non-gaming CD-ROM software, like education, interactive music and reference applications.

While 20th Century Fox was no stranger to games, licensing out the likes of The Simpsons and Aliens to other software houses, rumour was they were about to start their own games division.

According to a survey by BMRB International (who?) 76% of parents felt their kids spent too much time playing games, with 60% thinking it would have harmful consequences.

Karaoke specialist Arbiter were about to release 2,000 singalong CDs for the CD32. Really? 2,000?

The One recommended Bruce Sterling’s latest book, “The Hacker Crackdown”.

There was an apology for a couple of the Bumper Festive Competition’s anagrams being misprinted.

Notable Reviews

  • “Cool Spot” Virgin Interactive - 84% “Yes, you’ll be annoyed in places. Yes, you’ll scream and shout at the harshness of some of the jumps. But you’ll always come back for more. I suppose a lot of Cool Spot’s appeal is from Cool Spot himself.”
  • “The Labyrinth of Time” Terra Nova/Electronic Arts - 81% “Labyrinth of Time’s obvious selling point is the graphics. There can be no denying that without the advent of CDs this game would never have been possible, so as far as utilising the storage medium it scores very highly indeed. Unfortunately, the adventure itself doesn’t quite come across as being as impressive as the graphics.”
  • “Brian the Lion” Reflections/Psygnosis - 82% “I’d be lying if I said that I hadn’t deliberated long and hard over my overall mark for BTL because at the end of the day, for all the good points in the game, there’s still something missing.”
  • “Campaign 2” Jonathan Griffiths/Empire - 80% “The best part of Campaign II is that the campaign editor allows you to develop just about any combat scenario which ensures that you should never become bored with the game.”
  • “F1” Lankhor/Domark - 90% “It falls neatly between the two formal categories of driving games - i.e. the simulations (a la MicroProse’s Formula One) and the arcade games (a la Nigel Mansell’s World Championship) - containing the polygon graphics and accurate track modelling of the former and the indestructible cars, bouncing walls and linear road movement of the latter.”
  • “Goblins 3” Coktel Vision - 80% “Auditory recriminations aside, Goblins 3 (with or without the surplus ‘i’s) is a fine comic adventure romp. Its strengths are in the clever construction of the puzzles and the way they mesh perfectly with the storyline.”
  • “Fantastic Dizzy” Purple Haze/Codemasters - 84% “Ultimately it’s all very simple, but all I know is that I had a lot of fun playing this, and I’m sure even the real Dizzy haters (like I was!) will find something here to enjoy.”
  • “Simon the Sorcerer” Adventure Soft - 89% “At last! It seems like ages since the Amiga has been treated to a decent graphic adventure romp (Er, what about Beneath a Steel Sky, Matt? - David.) but now, thanks to Adventure Soft, it’s time once again to swap disks until you can’t swap no more.”
